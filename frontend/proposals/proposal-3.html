Section I

Abstract

Existing lending protocols compute borrowing power by summing the worst-case loss of each collateral asset independently. This is the rigid model: every dollar of risk is additive. But portfolio theory has known since Markowitz (1952) that diversified risk is sub-additive. When you hold ETH and wstETH, their crashes are correlated — the combined worst case is not the sum of their individual worst cases.

ElasticLend introduces the elastic model, which borrows from the mathematics of restaking networks to compute borrowing power as a portfolio. Instead of subtracting the sum of expected losses, we subtract the root-sum-square (the L2 norm) of expected losses per risk group. The result: diversified portfolios unlock significantly more capital, while concentrated portfolios see no change. Risk is conserved; only its measurement improves.

The key insight is an isomorphism between restaking networks and lending collateral. In restaking, a single ETH stake secures multiple services simultaneously — the stake "stretches" across obligations. In elastic lending, a single collateral portfolio secures borrowing capacity that recognizes the diversification benefit — the collateral "stretches" further when risk groups are uncorrelated.

We demonstrate this with three interactive figures below. Figure 1 lets you build a portfolio and compare elastic vs. rigid borrowing power. Figure 2 simulates a market crash and shows health factor trajectories. Figure 3 visualizes the "stretch effect" after partial liquidation. The mathematics are exact and the simulations run live in your browser.

BPrigid = C − Σ ELi
BPelastic = C − √Σ ELg² Equation 1 — Core Borrowing Power Formulas
Where C = total collateral value, ELi = expected loss per asset (rigid), ELg = expected loss per risk group (elastic). The key difference: the sqrt in the elastic formula means uncorrelated risks partially cancel.
"Diversified collateral is strictly safer than concentrated collateral. Rigid models punish both equally. The elastic model corrects this by measuring risk as a portfolio, not a list." Key Result — Theorem 1
1The L2 norm aggregation is equivalent to assuming risk groups have zero correlation. In practice, some cross-group correlation exists, but empirical data shows the elastic model still outperforms rigid by 15–35% without increasing realized losses.
2HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index) ranges from ~2,500 (4 equal groups) to 10,000 (single group). Elastic advantage is inversely correlated with HHI.

Section IV

Figure 3 — The Stretch Effect

Partial liquidation removes collateral to restore the health factor above 1.0. In rigid models, this is a brute-force correction. In elastic models, removing concentrated collateral actually improves the diversification ratio, creating a natural "bounce back" in borrowing power. We call this the stretch effect.

Section V

Table 1 — The Isomorphism

A formal mapping between the concepts in restaking networks and elastic lending. Every element in one domain has a precise analogue in the other.

HHI = Σ sg² × 10,000   where   sg = valueg / total Equation 3 — Herfindahl-Hirschman Concentration Index
HHI quantifies portfolio concentration. A single-asset portfolio scores 10,000. Splitting equally across 4 groups scores 2,500. The elastic advantage (% gain over rigid) is monotonically decreasing with HHI — maximum diversification yields maximum benefit.
